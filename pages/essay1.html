Should immigrants keep their names or change?

It was February, 2020. I was in a line of the immigration counter, Los Angeles airport with dreams about a new life and future, but it didn’t take long to burst my dream bubble and start to feel like I didn’t belong here.  I can still remember one incident that made me think so. During the immigration process at the airport, an officer checked my passport and looked straight at me and asked “are you Cha..mi..th?” He was trying so hard to get my name right. The pronunciation was totally unacceptable but I said yes. I didn’t want to be his headache for the next few minutes. Moreover, I didn’t want to be cringed by repeating my name out loud. But I realized that name is going to be a hex and future is going to be a bumpy ride. I wasn’t wrong. It happened over and over again. This is a very common struggle when moving to a new country. It is common for immigrants like me to suffer from language barriers and outlandishly sounding names. Language can be mastered over time but what about names? I noticed that some immigrants Were using western names and it ignited a series of struggles in my mind. Should I change the name? Or keep it? If I change it, what’s next? Is changing the name to something Western like “Charlie” going to fix my broken English and make me look like a native citizen? ...And… wait a second, my mom and dad didn’t have a son named “Charlie”. Where did this “Charlie” guy come from? Suddenly I started to feel like I was about to mess with my origins as well as my name. Also, I doubted how pretending to be like someone else affects my self-confidence. Then I decided keeping the original name is the way to go. I strongly suggest that immigrants should use their real name to keep their connection to their origin and culture, as well as to improve their self-confidence even though it is really challenging. However, having a name that is hard to pronounce could negatively impact on their academic or career growth. 


First and foremost, immigrants should keep using their national name because it represents their origin, culture and history. According to Wartanian in his article “Claiming the History in Family Names,” using the real name earns someone a chance to tell the story behind their name. Also, he or she can use names to pass down the history and inheritance of their old generations to the new generations (Wartanian, par. 3). In order to prove the fact, he explains that his family name “Vartan” means warrior and how his grandfather survived the Armenian Genocide just like a warrior. However, Wartanian’s father muted the conversation of their history by using a different Western name instead of his real name (Wartanian, par. 17). Just like Wartanian’s father, some people do not prefer to talk about their history even if they should be proud of it. I believe this behavior comes from the natural sense of safety. They might think staying quiet is the best approach to stay protected. In his childhood, Wartanian did not know anything about their history and it made him believe that having a Western name like Kevin would help him to fit into the society. However, he changed his mind once he got to know the legacy behind his name (Wartanian, par. 10).  The story can be attributed to many non-Western names throughout the world. They often have a connection to their heritage, culture, or origin. Changing names may help immigrants and future generations of their families to fit into their new society, but at the same time they have to segregate themselves from their legacy and descendants will never know about their origin. This is why I strongly suggest that immigrants should keep their names unchanged. 


Secondly, Immigrants should consider keeping their real name since using different names can negatively affect their self-confidence. There is a good example for this issue in the article “After being James, Peter, and William, I decided to stick with my Chinese name”, where Zheping Huang describes his feeling once he introduced himself to someone by a different name. Huang mentions, “I had to stick with James for a little over two years. Though I didn’t have many encounters with foreigners during that period, I still recall an exchange student from Edinburgh, named Fiona, looking unconvinced when I introduced myself as “James Huang.” Perhaps my introduction didn’t sound confident. If so, no wonder: I didn’t feel like a James.” (Huang, par .13). According to him, it is obvious that he made a weak first impression. People using several identifications might encounter conflicts. First, they have to remember both identities. Then they have to categorize all strangers in the society into groups and decide which name should be used for each group. Hence, every single time they have to make an introduction, they have to go through this process. It could take a little bit more time and hesitation, resulting in a weak and unconvincing introduction. The exact same thing happened to Huang. When the listener does not believe the very first words of someone, it ruins that person’s self-confidence. That is another good reason for immigrants to consider keeping their names unchanged.


As I discussed above, keeping names is great. But there are some inevitable drawbacks of keeping names. A person can simply ignore the mispronunciation of a random stranger. There is not the end of the world. But what if someone’s name categorizes that person into a detrimental group at the hiring process or academic process?  Sadly, this is a real-world scenario. Cody Delistraty in his article “Who wins in the name game?” has mentioned various facts that proves this stereotype. “A 2004 study showed that all else being equal, employers selected candidates with names like Emily Walsh and Greg Baker for callbacks almost 50 percent more often than candidates with names like Lakisha Washington and Jamal Jones” (Delistraty, par .11). Moreover, he mentioned the pronounceability of the name could overcome the work experience (Delistraty, par .11).  According to Delistraty’s further explanation on name's impact on education, teachers hold lower expectations for the students with non-western or Asian names (Delistraty, par .11). These facts clearly illustrate that there is a biasness for the name. I believe this is a version of a microaggression that started and fueled by smaller groups of people who were suffering from xenophobia. They use names as a tool to filter out the immigrants even without seeing them. I believe this is one legitimate reason that could make immigrants change their names.


As a result of globalization, it is very common to see vividly diversified communities in many countries. Especially here in the United States, you will not find someone who hasn't heard or used a non-western name. Also, I have noticed, the vast majority of people give honest effort on getting another person’s name right even though it is unfamiliar to them. Most of the time, they do not hesitate to apologize about any mispronunciations. That is why I strongly believe  immigrants should consider keeping their real names to keep up the connection with their culture and origin, as well as to maintain self-confidence by being real to themselves. I really wish I could sum up this story by only mentioning the optimistic aspect of keeping names. But the real-world prejudice that is built based on the name, don’t let me do that. Finally, I would say keep the name, but don’t let it take away your life’s opportunities. Each person should try really hard to find their own sweet spot between these two options. It would not be the same for everyone.
