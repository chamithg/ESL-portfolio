What are the greener and sustainable alternatives for generating electricity that can replace fossil fuel usage in the US?

I spent my childhood in a rural area in Sri Lanka, where people don't care much about infrastructure. People did not ask for water; they had their own water resources like wells and springs. Using firewood, people ignored liquid petroleum gas for cooking. There was one thing they couldn't do without, and that was electricity, just like the rest of the world. The necessity for electricity has grown rapidly over the years. In Sri Lanka, more than half of that electricity is coming from fossil fuel which costs millions of dollars each week.  Now Sri Lanka is experiencing its worst financial crisis of all time due to numerous administrative flaws, including the government having unqualified and unethical people handle the country's financial sector, as well as the pandemic. Now there is a shortage for most essentials like fossil fuel. Accordingly, the government has decided to implement power cuts for 4 - 8 hours each day. This situation has crippled the small businesses and day to day life of general the community. When People couldn't bear this anymore and their aggression has been turned into nationwide protests as it is today. Buying fossil fuels is not the only cause for that chaotic situation, but it is a major impact. Government could have reduced the pressure on countries foreign reserves if they have found a renewable energy source. The rest of the world may think that they are protected against power shortages as long as they have a strong enough economy to buy fuel. But I believe this could be any country of the world once the world runs out of fossil fuel.  I believe the whole world has recognized the need of transitioning to renewable power sources. The question is do they care enough? To find an answer for that question, it is best to look at a wealthy country that has money to invest in research like the United States rather than a country that is already suffering the consequences of not terminating its dependence on fossil fuels. When the public preference, government actions plans, and feasibility are considered, wind and solar will dominate renewable electricity production in the U.S.  


First and foremost, it is worth knowing the present statistics of electricity generation in the U.S. Electricity consumption is increasing rapidly in the US especially due to the Electric vehicle, the future of green transportation. Driving an EV, People enjoy the proud feeling of being able to take part in the green and clean air community.  But they are wrong and right at the same time. According to the article “Public Preferences in a Shifting Energy Future” by Hilary S Boudet and Shawn O Hazboun, 60 % of the electricity of the US is coming from burning fossil fuel (Natural gas and Coal). Then another 19% comes from nuclear energy and only 21% comes from renewable resources (Page. 2As a result, more fuel is burned to supply electricity to those clean air vehicles, which causes fuel reserves to dry out faster and more carbon dioxide to be emitted. Based on those facts, it is clear that the U.S. requires an alternative energy source. In the same article, U.S. electricity generation statistics are compared with those of Canada and there are some obvious differences between the two countries. Canada’s major portion of electricity comes from hydro power, which is 60% while the US only generates 7% of the total electricity requirement from water.  Canada only generates 17% of the electricity from Fossil fuels and they have set a huge step in the right direction when transitioning to renewable energy sources (Page. 2). Despite the fact of being neighbours globally, Canada and the United States have lots of climatic differences. For instance, many states of the U.S.  have dry climates. They do not get enough water to generate electricity and it is obvious that the U.S. can’t follow the footsteps of Canada regarding hydro electricity generation. They need to come up with a custom-tailored plan that fits the U.S. to step away from fossil fuels regarding electricity generation.


Predicting the direction of a nation requires knowing the preferences of the general public, especially in countries like the U.S. where citizens have the opportunity to vote on government decisions. People, however, tend to keep quiet as long as they can get electricity somehow. That is exactly what happened in Sri Lanka, the country that I mentioned earlier. Despite the fact of not having enough money to be spent on research, The country has enough resources that can be turned into hydroelectricity, solar power, wind power, and wave-tidal power. Unfortunately, people did not pay enough attention in transitioning to renewable energy sources. But, if considered the U.S. generally it seems that people do care, at least for reversing climate change or to relieve the skyrocketing gas prices. The Same reasons make people in the U.S. move toward electric vehicles. That is really a positive sign (even though the electricity does not come from fully greener sources.). What will their concern be about the source of electricity generation?  In “Public Preferences in a Shifting Energy Future'' by Hilary S Boudet and Shawn O Hazboun, the authors have provided official statistics on the people's preferences among all the sources of electricity generation. Those figures are based on several research done with the general public. According to those figures, solar energy is the highest demanded alternative and over 90% of the people have voted for increasing the usage of solar energy. The second place goes to wind energy with a preference percentage of 83% and wave-Tidal energy is at third place with 79% followed by Geothermal energy (69%) and Hydroelectricity (44%) (Page. 2). The personal opinions that I came up on this regard resonate with those numbers very closely. Solar and wind energy are excellent sources of energy despite the costs of installing the infrastructure. The Problem with most alternative energy resources is the fact that output could be very different from time to time. For example, you can't rely on solar energy for a whole year in a state like Washington. The sun is not shining over those states for the most part of the year.  There are some situations where green energy does not actually turn out to be green. That is why scientific research should be done together with the public opinion before making the movement.


Preference of the general public is only based on their knowledge about different power sources for the most part. However, the actual action plan could differ from those opinions when feasibility reports were considered. These discrepancies can be noticed when comparing those public preference figures to this National Geographic article, “A Blueprint for a Carbon-Free America.”  by Craig Welch. There, Welch discusses the methodology of the United States for elimination of fossil fuels and complete dependence on renewable energy by 2050. The article also includes the vision of Stanford professor Mark Jacobson vision in this regard. According to that, wind energy will be the primary source of electricity generation, which contributes 50 % of the national electricity generation. The second most used power source will be solar and it will contribute about 45% from total generation, hydroelectricity will be the third place contributing 3% and the rest will be coming from geothermal and tidal energy (Page. 3). Despite the fact of switching the order, Solar and wind will be the most used case just like preferred by the general public. Those figures were created assuming fossil fuel usage will be zero by then. Even the transportation will be solely dependent on electricity and Hydrogen. The reality, however, can be far from these research paper figures when it comes to stepping away from fossil fuels completely. The world is already suffering from difficulties finding materials for electric vehicle batteries.  So, the manufacturers will have to slow down the production day by day going forward. That fact alone is enough to prove that the world has to stick with fossil fuel for much longer than expected time frame. The figures they have mentioned about the hydro electrics sounds realistic because water resources are not expanding over the time but energy consumption does. Obviously, it's clear that solar energy and wind will play a major part in the renewable energy future and I don't see a problem short listing those two energy sources for further discussion.


Due to the existing evidence, wind energy and solar energy will be the primary renewable forms of electricity generation in the U.S. in the future. However, the feasibility of implementing those infrastructures may leave some room for uncertainty. When starting off considering solar energy, it is obvious that surface area that is covered by solar panels will be the most considerable factor and of course the consistent sunny weather. According to the forecast by Welch, the 45% electricity generation will be the sum of 7.2 % from Domestic- Rooftop solar panels, 30.7% from solar farms, 7.3% from concentrated industrial solar farms (Page. 3). Otherwise, they will need huge battery stations to store the power. This is really challenging, but there is a possible solution. In “Stanford researchers get solar panels to produce electricity without sunlight” by Jacob Bourne, reveals information about a solar panel produced by Stanford University which is capable of producing electricity at night (Par .1). This will be a huge game changer in the transitioning to renewable energy sources. Up next is the feasibility of Wind energy. The forecasts break down the predicted 50% of electricity that gets from Wind energy to 19.1% from offshore wind and 30.9 % from onshore wind. Apart from the cost of implementing wind turbines and power stations, it seems like there are not many obstacles. But that is not true. Wind turbines hugely affect the lifestyles of airborne animals. According to “An evaluation of bird and bat mortality at wind turbines in the Northeastern United States” by Daniel Y. Choi, Thomas W. Wittig and Bryan M. Kluever, the U.S. has reported annual bird fatalities ranging from 140,000 to 679,000 belongs to 300 species (page. 2). Also, those turbines need to be adjusted according to the wind direction and the speed it will be a lot of work especially considering offshore wind turbines. However, when comparing with other available energy sources, it is obvious that solar and wind energy check almost all of the boxes on practicality, renewability and ultimately feasibility.


In conclusion, the future of transitioning to renewable energy sources can be imagined by comparing present statistics to public awareness, government preference and feasibility of available energy sources. It is nice to have the same directional opinions from general public and scientific research and it sounds like a plan. But no one knows what kind of technology will be implemented tomorrow. The future will totally depend on those technologies and outcomes will be adjusted accordingly. However, I believe the richer countries like the U.S. should lead the competition by finding the solutions for a greener future, then other countries have a path with footsteps to follow along. Greener future for the world depends entirely on the adherence of the countries of the world to this collective responsibility.
