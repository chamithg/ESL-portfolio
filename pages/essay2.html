Is the English language an enemy or ally?

I spent most of my life in my home country Sri Lanka, which is a small tropical island and a main tourist attraction of the Asia region. Many tourists can be seen at any corner of my country. Whenever you see a group of tourists, you will see a native person standing next to them, trying to make an interaction with him. Locals conduct their own small, unofficial businesses which are related to tourism such as guiding them and selling souvenirs. They are extremely capable in marketing of their products and services. I really enjoyed watching how they interact with foreigners using a few English words they know. Most of those locals do not have a proper education. Sometimes they don't even know how to write their name in the native language, Sinhala. But they have picked up enough English words to interact with tourists and earn some money. For some people, that could be the only income source to keep food on the table for their families. But…Is the English language always this generous? it is something that needs a further analysis. This is a small example that demonstrates the good aspect of world dominance of English. English plays a major role for extending the opportunities beyond the local market and the global dominance of English fuels it even further. However, so-called English dominance crushes the many other languages by desaturating the linguistics diversity and vandalizing cultures.


First and foremost, we can't neglect the huge positive impact that is being delivered by the global dominance of English on extending career and business opportunities to the global level. English can be considered as a general medium of communication between different countries and several examples supporting this fact can be found in the article “Virtually Running America” by Robert McCrum. India's Call center industry is one of them. There McCrum describes how America’s call center jobs were outsourced to the employees from India. This industry started from zero and its growth skyrocketed within a couple of years. Lots of applicants were in a gold rush to snatch a job in a call center. These applicants were highly qualified graduates and most importantly they had to be fluent in English. There is no American or British English here, only Globish, which is a simplified version of global English. Ultimately, this call center situation resulted in an increased interest in learning English among young Indian generations. (McCrum par. 1 - 8).    This new industry can be considered a win-win situation for both countries. America can save money on wages and India gets lots of job opportunities. This is how globalization of English fuels developing new industries and opportunities. McCrum mentions another example related to Bollywood, which is India's movie industry. According to author, this industry also shows a boom when it comes to global popularity and now it has worldwide fan base of 3.6 million (McCrum par. 17 - 18). The main reason behind the success of this industry is its departure from tradition due to the linguistic and cultural change in India today. When Globish becomes more general in the local community, its influence changes the movie scenes accordingly. Bollywood movie producers also collaborate with Hollywood actors in Bollywood movies (McCrum par. 18). Now those movies are not only for the Indian community. Ultimately, Bollywood superstars have become more famous than Hollywood superstars. This is not a strange. We enjoy watching movies in different languages, but when those movies share a commonality with us, our interest grows even stronger. Globish is that shared commonality. Those examples on India's call centers and film industry demonstrate two of the positive aspects of English's global dominance related to career growth.


Even though English is so important to communicate internationally, its dominance crushes all other languages by desaturating the linguistics diversity. Just like the sun makes stars invisible during day time, English dominance shines over other languages and decreases their popularity. In “Behemoth, Bully, Thief: How the English Language is Taking over the Planet” by Jacob Mikanowski, He mentioned “It is aspirational: the golden ticket to the worlds of education and international commerce, a parent’s dream and a student’s misery, winnower of the haves from the have-nots. It is inescapable: the language of global business, the internet, science, diplomacy, stellar navigation, avian pathology. And everywhere it goes, it leaves behind a trail of dead: dialects crushed, languages forgotten, literature mangled.” to describe the negative aspects of the global dominance of English (Mikanowski par. 4). Nowadays this world depends on the concept of learning English as the only way to enter the competitive world. Rwanda had the same idea, so they switched their national language and educational system to English from French in 2008. (Mikanowski par. 10). They certainly contributed a lot to the death of their native language through this geopolitical action. In terms of individual actions, parents tend to push their children to learn English, which separates them from their native tongue. children do not have a choice but to learn English and it drastically discourages the growth and existence of all other languages and literature. Mikanowski also points out that in South Korea, parents often have their children undergo tongue surgeries to improve their English pronunciation. (Mikanowski par. 12). It is unacceptable that there is such a bias towards English. Learning English is great. But it should be optional. I think the best way to figure out this is to let children decide whether they need to learn English or not. The native language should be carried forward together with the English. That is the only way to prevent the English 'Behemoth' from desaturating the linguistics diversity.


            Global English dominance has also resulted in cultural vandalism in addition to the effects on linguistic diversity. English language also carries the western cultural aspect such as western fashions, western sociological behaviors and many more western things. Sometimes they don't collaborate with native cultures of non-western countries. I can turn back to my home country, Sri Lanka, to tell what exactly this is. Colombo is the capital of Sri Lanka and it is located on the western side of the country. The people who live in that area take being “western side” very seriously and they try to use English as much as possible. Their fashion is western, their language is English, and their behavior is western. Some parents do not even bother to teach the native language Sinhala and traditional things to their children. You will find several places like shops and high-end hotels where you can't get anything done using the national language, Sinhala. They basically use English to define the class of the people. They call themselves “Cool and Posh” just because they know some English words. Some locals who don't speak English suffer from this cultural barrier in the western part of the country. This is a cultural disaster in my perspective. On the other hand, I have seen many Sri Lankan- American parents who are trying to teach Sinhala to their American born children to maintain the connection to their origins. They respect being bilingual. I don't know why western Sri Lankan people can't figure this out. I believe Sri Lanka is not the only place that native language is boycotted. There is an example that resonates with this in the article “how to tame a wild tongue” by Gloria Anzaldua. The author mentioned, “Chicanas feel uncomfortable talking in Spanish to Latinas, afraid of their judgment. Their language was not outlawed in their countries. They had a whole lifetime of being immersed in their native tongue; generations, centuries in which Spanish was a first language, taught in school, heard on the radio and on TV, and read in the newspaper.” (Anzaldua par.17). Here she explains the situation of native American- Spanish people (Chicanas), when it comes to freedom of using their language by comparing it to Latinas, who were not banned from speaking Spanish. She expresses her outrage at having her mother tongue banned in her native land. Just like Sri Lanka. I know, this cultural disaster is not something directly delivered by English, but the attitude of the people. But we can't ignore that English is the root of it. That is why I strongly believe that English vandalizes cultures.


In conclusion it is very common to see that global English dominance is highly effective in crushing other languages and vandalizing cultures despite being helpful for enhancing career opportunities and economic growth. So, is it an Enemy? Yes and no. It really depends on the way people use it. People should use English for their personal or career development but they shouldn't let English define them and take them away from their cultures and traditions. English can’t possess people if they have pride and respect for their own language and cultures. If they let those pride and respect fade away, English can easily squeeze in. English is like a sharp knife. It can be a useful tool or it can hurt the user. So my recommendation is to use the tool with caution. That is the only way to stop English from crushing other languages and vandalizing cultures.
